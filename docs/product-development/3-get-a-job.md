
## Elevator pitch

- **audience**: choose one job position to describe yourself as
	- *example*: not "investor, comedian, and software engineer," but just "software engineer"
- **credential**: your credentials
- **experience**: relevant experience and what you did
- **value**: any valuable results or outcomes of your experience
- **personal**: personal hobbies that showcase something unique about you
- **conclusion**: what you want out of this conversation, 
	- **example**: "I'm currently looking for jobs in software engineering"

## Interviews

### Before the interview

1. Research the company
2. Research the company history
3. Research the industry
4. Based off the first three research tasks, **make questions** about the indistry and the specific company's business strategy.

>"Hey netflix, I research your company and your competitors, so I'm wondering your strategy to go against paramount and disney+"

#### Answers to prepare

For each of these categories, make 2-3 answers and prepare them beforehand

- **problem solving story**
- **team or conflict story**
- **leadership or initiative story**
### During the interview

#### What interviewers look for

- **are you a good person I would enjoy being with?**: Interviewers will only hire you if they like you, meaning that you would be someone they enjoy working with.
- **are you a good communicator?**: If you're rambling, then they don't trust you to convey ideas clearly and then will not hire you.

#### Tips

1. **ask good questions**: good questions 1) benefit you and 2) come from company research
2. **call them by their names**: psychological tip that helps build familiarity and makes the interviewers feel like you care them.
3. **follow calm, confident, curious**
	- **confident**: own your strengths and weaknesses
	- **calm**: It's ok to pause to think about a question, don't keep rambling. Don't use filler words, and think about a question and just ask, "can you give me a minute to think?"
	- **curious**: show genuine interest in the role and ask good questions, turns an interview into an interrogation to see if the job is the right fit for you

#### Asking questions

During an interview, **you must ask good questions**. It is a red flag for interviewers if they don't hear you asking questions. 

Do not just ask questions for the sake of asking questions. Good questions consist of two components:

1. **it benefits you**: Dumb questions do not benefit you. Good questions do benefit you. Do not just ask questions for the sake of asking questions.
2. **comes from previous research**: Good questions are based off researching the company beforehand.

> You judge a person by their questions, not their answers - Voltaire

#### Managing nerves

Nerves as normal. Reframe them as energy, focus, and readiness.

Mini tips:

- deep breath
- pause
- speak slower

#### Good answers

Here are good examples of good answers

- **motivation**

- **skills**
	- **strong**: I demonstrated x by doing Y and achieving Z

#### STAR method

> "Facts tell, stories sell"

Nobody cares if you give them the facts. They want a story. They want to feel something.

- Interviewers remember stories, not resume,
- stories show how you think and act
- stories prove impact

## Communications

- **why smart people get overlooked**: never overload people with technical jargon. Here are the red flags why smart people get overlooked
	- **Too much jargon**: keep in mind your audience
	- Rambling and over-explaining
	- **No clear structure**: Do not deliver information as a stream of consciousness. There should be a clear structure to communication.
- **clarity creates opportunity**: one clear message that conveys your intentions beats ten scattered ideas
- **communicate with confidence**: Always speak slowly, and pause for thought so you can clam your nerves


### Communicating your value

Here is the number one tip:

>Before entering any professional conversation, ask yourself what is the one thing I want them to remember.

For example, do you want them to remember how you will change the trajectory of their company to make it better than their competitors?

There are three components of communicating your value to a recruiter:

- **responsibilities**: what did you do?
- **results**: what were the results of what you did?
- **business value**: what value did your results give to the business?
